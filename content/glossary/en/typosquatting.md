**Typosquatting** (also called URL hijacking) is a specific form of [cybersquatting](/en/glossary/cybersquatting/) in which a registrant registers common misspellings, transpositions, or near-homoglyphs of a high-traffic domain — for example, replacing one letter, adding a hyphen, or swapping a character for a lookalike — to intercept the [type-in traffic](/en/glossary/type-in-traffic/) that flows from users who mistype the intended address. The captured visitors are then monetized through pay-per-click ads, redirected to a competing service, or directed to a [phishing](/en/glossary/phishing/) page designed to harvest credentials. Most typosquatting of trademarked brand names is actionable under the [UDRP](/en/glossary/udrp/) or the US [ACPA](/en/glossary/acpa/), which both cover confusingly similar registrations. Tokenizing a typosquatted domain does not change its legal exposure; on-chain domain services are built on real DNS registrations, which remain subject to standard dispute mechanisms. Savvy brand owners register their own common typos defensively and point them to the canonical domain.
