**Domain theft** is the end result of a successful [domain hijacking](/en/glossary/domain-hijacking/) attack: the name has been moved, via an unauthorized transfer, to a registrar or account controlled by the attacker, severing the rightful owner's access entirely. The attack typically begins with account compromise — through [phishing](/en/glossary/phishing/), leaked credentials, or social engineering of [registrar](/en/glossary/registrar/) support — followed by disabling protections and submitting a transfer using the [auth-code](/en/glossary/auth-code/) obtained from inside the account. Recovery is difficult and slow: [ICANN](/en/glossary/icann/) dispute procedures can take months, and in the interim the stolen domain may resolve to malicious content or be transferred again. The best defenses are layered prevention: keep the [transfer lock](/en/glossary/transfer-lock/) enabled, use strong unique credentials with hardware MFA, and register high-value names under registry-lock programs.
