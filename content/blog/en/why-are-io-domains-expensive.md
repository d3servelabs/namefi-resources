If you have priced a `.io` domain lately and winced, you are not alone. Compared with a plain `.com` or one of the cheaper new extensions, `.io` consistently lands in the premium tier — both to register and, more painfully, to renew every year.

## The Short Answer

**`.io` domains commonly cost more to register and renew because the [registry](/en/glossary/registry/) wholesale price is relatively high, while demand for short names and the "input/output" association can raise secondary-market prices.** Retail prices still vary by registrar, term, premium status, promotion, and currency, so compare the current registration and renewal quote for the exact name.

In other words, the cost is part **[wholesale pricing](/en/glossary/wholesale-pricing/)**, part **demand and prestige**, and part **scarcity**. Below, we unpack each driver — and a newer geopolitical wrinkle that some buyers now weigh.

## 1. Registry Wholesale Pricing Is Simply Higher

Every domain has two price layers: the **wholesale price** the registry charges [registrars](/en/glossary/registrar/), and the **retail markup** your registrar adds. For `.io`, the wholesale floor sits well above commodity extensions.

IANA's current [delegation record](https://www.iana.org/domains/root/db/io.html) names **Internet Computer Bureau Limited** as the `.io` sponsoring organization. Identity Digital identifies ICB as part of its registry portfolio. Unlike `.com`, whose registry agreement constrains wholesale pricing, `.io` pricing is set under a different ccTLD arrangement. A registrar notice documented a **$7 USD wholesale increase effective January 16, 2026**; that is a completed adjustment, not a future schedule. Historical list-price, rebate, and retail changes should not be collapsed into one unsupported increase timeline.

Wholesale changes affect registrar costs, but retail prices need not move by the same amount or on the same date. Registrar markup, promotions, currency, tax, and premium-name rules can still produce meaningful differences, especially between first-year registration and renewal.

## 2. Intense Startup, VC, and Developer Demand

Demand also matters, especially in technology categories where buyers recognize `.io` as the abbreviation for input/output.

For engineers, `.io` reads as **I/O — input/output**, a foundational computing concept. That association has made the ccTLD a recognizable branding option for:

- **SaaS and AI startups** signaling a technology focus
- **Web3 and blockchain projects** (think block explorers and dApps)
- **Developers** hosting portfolios, docs, and projects (GitHub Pages famously uses `github.io`)
- **Gaming**, thanks to the wave of browser-based "`.io` games"

That buyer interest can increase competition for concise labels. It does not guarantee a sale price or make the extension credible to every audience, but it can support higher asking prices for names that multiple buyers want.

## 3. Scarcity of Good Short Names Drives Secondary-Market Premiums

Many concise `.io` labels are already registered. When a desired label is held by someone else, the buyer is negotiating on the secondary market rather than paying the registry's standard registration price.

`.io` rewards **short, clever, brandable names**, including "domain hacks" where the extension becomes part of the word (`rad.io`, `stud.io`). The pool of genuinely great two- to six-letter names is tiny and largely picked over. When you want one of those, you are no longer buying from the registry — you are buying from a **previous owner on the secondary market**, where prices reflect what a startup or investor will pay, not a registrar's price list.

This is the same general supply-and-demand mechanism seen in other extensions: a particular registered label has one current registrant, and a resale price depends on the parties, comparable sales, use case, and willingness to transact.

## 4. Renewal Costs Add Up

The sticker shock on `.io` is not just the first year. Renewal pricing is recurring and can differ materially from a discounted first-year offer. A portfolio holder should budget from the registrar's current **renewal** quote, not the promotional registration price, and allow for future price changes without assuming a fixed annual increase pattern.

For a single brand domain this may be manageable. For anyone holding a **portfolio** of `.io` names, recurring renewals are a material operating cost. Some registrars support multi-year renewal, but eligible terms and the price applied to added years vary; confirm the exact quote and registry maximum term rather than assuming prepayment locks today's rate.

## 5. The New Factor: Chagos Sovereignty Uncertainty

Here is a driver that did not exist a few years ago, and which thoughtful buyers now factor in.

`.io` is technically the **country-code TLD ([ccTLD](/en/glossary/cctld/))** assigned to the **British Indian Ocean Territory (BIOT)** — the Chagos Archipelago. In May 2025 the UK and Mauritius **signed a treaty** to transfer sovereignty over the islands. Because ccTLDs are tied to the ISO 3166-1 country-code list, a change to BIOT's status raised a question: *what happens to `.io`?*

It is important to be **measured and accurate** here:

- **No `.io` deletion is happening or scheduled.** The UK and Mauritius signed the treaty in May 2025, but the UK implementing bill did not receive Royal Assent before the 2024–26 parliamentary session ended on April 29, 2026 and will make no further progress in that session. `.io` continues to operate normally.
- **ICANN has signaled there is no automatic kill switch.** Its policies provide for a long, **phased transition** — typically measured in *years* — if a ccTLD ever needs to be retired, not an overnight shutdown. ([ICANN](https://www.icann.org/en/blogs/details/the-chagos-archipelago-and-the-io-domain-14-11-2024-en))
- **Any change would be gradual.** ISO and ICANN would need to act in sequence, and even then existing registrations would not vanish without an extended wind-down.

So the realistic takeaway is **uncertainty, not a scheduled shutdown**. Future treaty implementation, legislation, ISO treatment, and ICANN action cannot be predicted from the failed 2024–26 bill alone. A buyer can account for that unresolved ccTLD-specific risk without assigning it an unsupported probability or deadline.

## Is a .io Domain Worth It for You?

A higher price is only a problem if you are not getting matching value. Ask yourself:

- **Does your audience understand the association?** A SaaS, AI, developer-tool, or Web3 audience may recognize `.io` as "input/output," but the extension does not guarantee credibility, traffic, or conversion.
- **Is the exact label available on acceptable terms?** Compare the complete `.io` and `.com` options, acquisition cost, renewal cost, and audience response instead of assuming one extension has better short-name availability.
- **Can you absorb renewal changes?** Budget for the quoted annual renewal cost and the possibility of future registry or registrar price changes.
- **How do you treat unresolved ccTLD policy risk?** If a flagship brand requires the most predictable policy path available, compare `.io` with gTLD alternatives and make an explicit risk decision.

If `.io` fits the audience and the total recurring cost fits the budget, it may be a reasonable choice. If lower cost or a gTLD policy path matters more, compare alternatives.

### Alternatives Worth Comparing

- **`.com`** — a widely recognized gTLD with contractually constrained wholesale pricing; short names can be unavailable or expensive on the secondary market.
- **`.ai`** — the breakout extension for artificial-intelligence brands. Note it is *also* a ccTLD (for Anguilla) and is itself **premium-priced**, so it solves the prestige question but not the cost or ccTLD-origin one. See our [`.ai` vs `.io` comparison](/en/blog/ai-vs-io-domain/) and the [`.ai` TLD guide](/en/tld/ai/).
- **`.dev`** — a true generic TLD operated by Google, developer-friendly, HTTPS-enforced, with no country-code dependency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are .io domains so expensive?

`.io` domains commonly have higher registration and renewal quotes because the registry wholesale cost is relatively high. Demand for the "input/output" association and competition for concise registered labels can also raise particular secondary-market prices, but retail and resale prices vary by name, registrar, term, and transaction.

### Why are .io renewals so costly?

`.io` renewal quotes commonly exceed cheaper legacy or promotional registration offers because the registry wholesale cost is relatively high and registrars add their own pricing. A documented $7 USD wholesale increase took effect in January 2026, but compare the current registrar renewal quote rather than relying on an article's historical price timeline.

### Why do startups use .io domains?

Engineers may read `.io` as I/O (input/output), so the extension can signal a technology focus to an audience that recognizes the association. It does not guarantee credibility or availability; compare the exact label, price, and audience fit with alternatives.

### Will .io domains be deleted because of the Chagos treaty?

No deletion is happening or scheduled, and `.io` operates normally. The UK implementing bill did not reach Royal Assent before the 2024–26 parliamentary session ended and will make no further progress in that session. If ISO and ICANN ever determine that retirement is required, ICANN policy provides a phased process rather than an overnight deletion; future treaty and legislative steps remain uncertain.

### Is a .io domain worth the higher price?

That depends on the exact label, current registration or acquisition price, recurring renewal cost, audience fit, and treatment of ccTLD policy risk. Compare those factors with `.com`, `.dev`, and other eligible alternatives.

### What are good alternatives to a .io domain?

Common alternatives include `.com` (a widely recognized gTLD with contractually constrained wholesale pricing), `.ai` (an Anguilla ccTLD used for AI branding, often with higher pricing), and `.dev` (a developer-focused generic TLD operated by Google Registry).
